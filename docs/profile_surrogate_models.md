# Profile Surrogate Models

## Overview

In variational integration of crystallographic diffraction data, each observed shoebox contains a Bragg reflection whose spatial intensity distribution — the **profile** — must be inferred. The profile is a probability vector $\mathbf{p} \in \Delta^{K-1}$ on the $K = D \times H \times W$ pixel simplex (e.g. $K = 1323$ for a $3 \times 21 \times 21$ shoebox).

The profile surrogate defines the variational posterior $q(\mathbf{p} \mid x)$ over profiles, conditioned on the encoder output $x$. Different surrogate architectures trade off between expressiveness, interpretability, and the magnitude of the KL divergence to the prior — which directly affects the tightness of the ELBO and hence the quality of integrated intensities.

This document describes the three profile surrogate families implemented in the codebase.

---

## 1. The Logistic-Normal Latent Profile Model

All three surrogates share a common generative structure:

$$
\mathbf{p} = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{W} \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b})
$$

where:
- $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a low-dimensional latent vector ($d \ll K$)
- $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ is a **decoder matrix** (basis)
- $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ is a **bias** (log-space reference profile)
- The softmax ensures $\mathbf{p}$ lies on the simplex

The variational posterior over $\mathbf{h}$ is a diagonal Gaussian:

$$
q(\mathbf{h} \mid x) = \mathcal{N}\bigl(\boldsymbol{\mu}_h(x),\; \mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_h(x)^2)\bigr)
$$

where $\boldsymbol{\mu}_h(x)$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_h(x)$ are predicted by two linear heads from the encoder output:

```
mu_h  = Linear(encoder_out, d)(x)           # (B, d)
std_h = softplus(Linear(encoder_out, d)(x))  # (B, d), positive
```

Profile samples are generated by the reparameterization trick:

$$
\mathbf{h} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_h + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_h \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})
$$

$$
\mathbf{p} = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{W} \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b})
$$

The three surrogates differ in:
1. How $\mathbf{W}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are obtained (learned vs. fixed)
2. The prior $p(\mathbf{h})$ (global vs. per-bin)

### Why this works

The softmax-linear model can be understood as saying: "the log-profile lives near a $d$-dimensional affine subspace of $\mathbb{R}^K$." Since $K = 1323$ but real Bragg profiles are described by a few parameters (position, width, shape), the effective dimensionality is much smaller. Typically $d = 8\text{--}30$ suffices, compared to the full $K$-dimensional Dirichlet which has 200–500 nats of KL.

## 2. Learned Basis (`LinearProfileSurrogate`)

**Class:** `LinearProfileSurrogate`
**Config name:** `linear_profile_surrogate`

### Architecture

Both $\mathbf{W}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are **learned parameters** (an `nn.Linear` layer):

```python
self.decoder = nn.Linear(latent_dim, output_dim)
# W = decoder.weight   (K, d) — trainable
# b = decoder.bias     (K,)   — trainable
```

The network jointly learns:
- The encoder: shoebox → encoder output $x$
- The latent heads: $x \to (\boldsymbol{\mu}_h, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_h)$
- The decoder: what the $d$ latent dimensions "mean" in profile space

### Prior

A single **global isotropic Gaussian**:

$$
p(\mathbf{h}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\; \sigma_p^2 \mathbf{I})
$$

with $\sigma_p$ typically set to 3.0. The KL divergence has the standard closed-form:

$$
\mathrm{KL}\bigl(q(\mathbf{h}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{h})\bigr) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \left[ \frac{\sigma_{h,j}^2}{\sigma_p^2} + \frac{\mu_{h,j}^2}{\sigma_p^2} - 1 - \ln\frac{\sigma_{h,j}^2}{\sigma_p^2} \right]
$$

### Strengths and limitations

**Strengths:**
- Maximally flexible: the decoder learns whatever basis best reconstructs the data
- Low KL (~15–30 nats in $d = 8$), much less than Dirichlet (~200–500 nats on $\Delta^{1322}$)
- Simple to configure: only needs `input_dim`, `latent_dim`, `output_dim`, `sigma_prior`

**Limitations:**
- The learned $\mathbf{W}$ has no physical interpretation — the latent dimensions are arbitrary
- The global prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_p^2 \mathbf{I})$ ignores the fact that profiles vary systematically with resolution and detector position
- Since $\mathbf{W}$ changes during training, per-bin priors in the latent space would be meaningless (the coordinate system is moving)

### Config example

```yaml
surrogates:
  qp:
    name: linear_profile_surrogate
    args:
      input_dim: 64
      latent_dim: 8
      output_dim: 1323    # D * H * W
      sigma_prior: 3.0
```

## 3. Fixed Hermite Basis (`PerBinLogisticNormalSurrogate` with `basis_type: hermite`)

**Class:** `PerBinLogisticNormalSurrogate`
**Config name:** `per_bin_logistic_normal`

### Motivation

To give physical meaning to the latent space and enable per-bin priors, we fix $\mathbf{W}$ to a known set of basis functions. The **Hermite function basis** uses products of Hermite polynomials with a Gaussian envelope — a natural choice because Bragg profiles are approximately Gaussian, and Hermite functions form a complete orthogonal system that efficiently captures deviations from Gaussianity.

### Construction

Each basis function (in 2D) is:

$$
\varphi_{n_x, n_y}(x, y) = H_{n_x}\!\left(\frac{x - c_x}{\sigma}\right) \cdot H_{n_y}\!\left(\frac{y - c_y}{\sigma}\right) \cdot \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - c_x)^2 + (y - c_y)^2}{4\sigma^2}\right)
$$

where:
- $H_n$ are the probabilist's Hermite polynomials, computed by the three-term recurrence $H_n(x) = x H_{n-1}(x) - (n-1) H_{n-2}(x)$
- $(c_x, c_y)$ is the shoebox center
- $\sigma$ is the reference Gaussian width (`sigma_ref`, typically 3.0 pixels)
- The envelope $\exp(-r^2 / 4\sigma^2)$ is the square root of the reference Gaussian — this makes the basis functions orthogonal in unweighted $L^2(\mathbb{R}^2)$ (Construction C)

The $(0, 0)$ mode (pure Gaussian) is excluded because it is absorbed into the bias $\mathbf{b}$. With `max_order = 4`, the remaining modes give $d = 14$ basis functions in 2D.

**Physical interpretation of the leading modes:**

| Mode $(n_x, n_y)$ | Physical meaning |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| $(1, 0)$ | Horizontal shift of the peak |
| $(0, 1)$ | Vertical shift of the peak |
| $(2, 0)$ | Horizontal width change |
| $(0, 2)$ | Vertical width change |
| $(1, 1)$ | Diagonal shear / tilt |
| $(3, 0), (0, 3)$ | Skewness |
| $(2, 2)$ | Kurtosis / peakedness |

### 3D extension

For 3D shoeboxes ($D > 1$ frames), the basis is extended as a product over the frame direction:

$$
\varphi_{n_x, n_y, n_z}(x, y, z) = H_{n_x}\!\left(\frac{x}{\sigma_{xy}}\right) H_{n_y}\!\left(\frac{y}{\sigma_{xy}}\right) H_{n_z}\!\left(\frac{z}{\sigma_z}\right) \cdot \mathrm{envelope}
$$

The maximum frame-direction order is capped at $\min(1, D-1)$ because $D$ is typically small (3 frames). This gives:
- $n_z = 0$: all spatial modes (same as 2D)
- $n_z = 1$: spatial modes × frame-linear (profile evolution across frames)

With `max_order = 4` and $D = 3$: $d = 29$ basis functions total.

### Bias vector

The bias $\mathbf{b}$ is the log of a normalized reference Gaussian:

$$
\mathbf{b} = \log\left(\frac{g(\mathbf{r})}{\sum_{\mathbf{r}'} g(\mathbf{r}')}\right), \qquad g(\mathbf{r}) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{c}|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

When $\mathbf{h} = \mathbf{0}$, the profile reduces to $\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{b})$ = the reference Gaussian.

### Per-bin prior

The key advantage over the learned basis: because $\mathbf{W}$ is fixed, the latent coordinates have a stable meaning throughout training. This allows us to define **per-bin priors** by projecting empirical profiles into the latent space:

1. **Background-subtract** all shoebox counts (quietest-frame method for 3D, border-pixel average for 2D)
2. **Normalize** to proportions and take the log: $\mathbf{z}_i = \log(\mathbf{p}_i + \epsilon)$
3. **Project** into latent space: $\mathbf{h}_i = (\mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{b})^\top \mathbf{W}$
4. For each bin $k$ (resolution shell, or resolution × azimuthal sector):
   - $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \mathrm{mean}(\{\mathbf{h}_i : i \in \text{bin } k\})$
   - $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_k = \mathrm{std}(\{\mathbf{h}_i : i \in \text{bin } k\})$, floored at 0.1

The prior for reflections in bin $k$ is:

$$
p_k(\mathbf{h}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\; \mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_k^2))
$$

The KL is still closed-form:

$$
\mathrm{KL}\bigl(q(\mathbf{h}) \,\|\, p_k(\mathbf{h})\bigr) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \left[ \frac{\sigma_{h,j}^2}{\sigma_{k,j}^2} + \frac{(\mu_{h,j} - \mu_{k,j})^2}{\sigma_{k,j}^2} - 1 - \ln\frac{\sigma_{h,j}^2}{\sigma_{k,j}^2} \right]
$$

This gives per-bin structure awareness (the prior "knows" that high-resolution profiles are broader, or that profiles shift with azimuthal angle) while keeping KL in the low-dimensional latent space (~15–30 nats).

### Config example

```yaml
surrogates:
  qp:
    name: per_bin_logistic_normal
    args:
      input_dim: 64
      basis_path: profile_basis_per_bin.pt

loss:
  args:
    profile_basis_per_bin: profile_basis_per_bin.pt
    profile_basis_type: hermite
    profile_basis_max_order: 4    # d ≈ 14 (2D) or 29 (3D)
    profile_basis_sigma_ref: 3.0  # envelope width in pixels
```

## 4. Fixed PCA Basis (`PerBinLogisticNormalSurrogate` with `basis_type: pca`)

**Class:** `PerBinLogisticNormalSurrogate` (same class, different basis file)
**Config name:** `per_bin_logistic_normal`

### Motivation

While the Hermite basis is physics-motivated and universal, it may not be the most *efficient* basis for a particular dataset. PCA finds the directions of maximum variance in log-profile space, giving the most compact representation for a given $d$.

### Construction

1. **Background-subtract** all profiles (same as Hermite)
2. **Normalize** to proportions: $\tilde{\mathbf{p}}_i = \mathbf{s}_i / \|\mathbf{s}_i\|_1$
3. **Log-transform**: $\mathbf{z}_i = \log(\tilde{\mathbf{p}}_i + \epsilon)$
4. **Center**: $\mathbf{b} = \bar{\mathbf{z}}$, $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i = \mathbf{z}_i - \mathbf{b}$
5. **SVD**: $\tilde{\mathbf{Z}} = \mathbf{U} \mathbf{S} \mathbf{V}^\top$
6. **Truncate**: $\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{V}_{1:d}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ (top $d$ right singular vectors)

The explained variance fraction for each component is $\lambda_j = S_j^2 / \sum_k S_k^2$.

### Bias vector

$\mathbf{b}$ is the mean of the log-profiles across the dataset. When $\mathbf{h} = \mathbf{0}$, the profile is $\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{b})$ — the "average" profile shape.

### Per-bin prior

Identical to the Hermite case: project all profiles, compute per-bin $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_k)$. The `profile_basis_per_bin.pt` file has the same format for both basis types.

### Trade-offs vs Hermite

| | PCA | Hermite |
|---|-----|---------|
| **Efficiency** | Maximally efficient — $d$ components capture the most variance possible | May need more components for the same explained variance |
| **Interpretability** | Components are abstract (linear combinations of pixel variations) | Each component has physical meaning (shift, width, skew, ...) |
| **Universality** | Dataset-specific — must be recomputed for each dataset | Universal — same basis works for any monochromatic dataset with similar shoebox geometry |
| **Robustness** | Sensitive to dataset composition (outliers, systematic errors) | Robust — basis is defined analytically |
| **Typical $d$** | 8–15 | 14 (2D) or 29 (3D) for max_order=4 |

### Config example

```yaml
surrogates:
  qp:
    name: per_bin_logistic_normal
    args:
      input_dim: 64
      basis_path: profile_basis_per_bin.pt

loss:
  args:
    profile_basis_per_bin: profile_basis_per_bin.pt
    profile_basis_type: pca
    profile_basis_d: 10           # number of PCA components
```

## 5. Comparison Summary

| Property | Learned (`linear_profile_surrogate`) | Hermite (`per_bin_logistic_normal`) | PCA (`per_bin_logistic_normal`) |
|----------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Decoder $\mathbf{W}$ | Learned (trainable) | Fixed (Hermite functions) | Fixed (PCA components) |
| Bias $\mathbf{b}$ | Learned (trainable) | $\log(\text{ref Gaussian})$ | Mean of log-profiles |
| Latent dim $d$ | 8 (configurable) | 14 (2D) / 29 (3D) | 8–15 (configurable) |
| Prior | Global $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_p^2 \mathbf{I})$ | Per-bin $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_k^2))$ | Per-bin $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_k^2))$ |
| KL magnitude | ~15–30 nats | ~15–30 nats | ~15–30 nats |
| Per-bin aware | No | Yes | Yes |
| Interpretable latent | No | Yes | Partially |
| Dataset-specific | No (learns from data) | No (universal) | Yes (must recompute) |

### When to use which

- **Learned basis**: Good baseline. Use when you don't need per-bin priors or interpretability. Simplest to configure.
- **Hermite basis**: Recommended for monochromatic data. Physics-motivated, interpretable, universal across datasets. Per-bin priors capture resolution and azimuthal dependence.
- **PCA basis**: Use when you want maximal compression efficiency for a specific dataset, or when profiles deviate substantially from Gaussian (e.g., polychromatic Laue data with streaky profiles).

## 6. 2D Binning

Both the Hermite and PCA surrogates support **2D profile binning** (resolution × azimuthal angle), configured via:

```yaml
loss:
  args:
    profile_binning:
      max_azi_bins: 4
      min_per_bin: 10
      beam_center: [2058.5, 2191.5]
```

This creates adaptive 2D bins: each resolution shell is subdivided into azimuthal sectors around the beam center. The number of sectors per shell is reduced until every bin has at least `min_per_bin` reflections (outer shells with sparse detector coverage automatically get fewer sectors).

The physical motivation: profile shape depends on both **resolution** (higher resolution → broader profiles due to mosaicity convolution) and **azimuthal angle** (profiles shift and distort based on detector geometry — the parallax effect). 2D binning captures both effects.

Without `profile_binning`, the priors use 1D resolution bins only (the `group_labels` computed from d-spacing).

## 7. Automatic Basis Generation

The `profile_basis_per_bin.pt` file is **auto-generated** by `prepare_per_bin_priors()` before training. When the file is referenced in `loss.args.profile_basis_per_bin` but doesn't exist, the preparation step:

1. Loads raw shoebox counts and masks
2. Background-subtracts all reflections
3. Builds the basis ($\mathbf{W}$, $\mathbf{b}$) according to `profile_basis_type`
4. Projects all profiles into latent space
5. Computes per-bin $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_k)$ using the profile group labels (1D or 2D)
6. Saves the complete basis file

To regenerate (e.g., after changing `max_order` or `basis_type`), delete `profile_basis_per_bin.pt` and re-run.

The saved file contains:
```python
{
    "W": Tensor,           # (K, d) basis matrix
    "b": Tensor,           # (K,) bias
    "d": int,              # latent dimensionality
    "mu_per_group": Tensor, # (n_bins, d) per-bin prior means
    "std_per_group": Tensor,# (n_bins, d) per-bin prior stds
    "sigma_prior": float,  # global fallback std
    "basis_type": str,     # "hermite_per_bin" or "pca_per_bin"
    "orders": list | None, # Hermite: list of (nx, ny[, nz]) tuples
    "explained_var": Tensor | None,  # PCA: per-component variance fraction
}
```

## 8. Mathematical Appendix: Why Fixed $\mathbf{W}$ Enables Per-Bin Priors

With a **learned** decoder $\mathbf{W}$, the latent coordinates $\mathbf{h}$ have no fixed meaning — they are defined only up to the current state of $\mathbf{W}$ during training. Per-bin statistics $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_k)$ computed from data before training would be in a coordinate system that $\mathbf{W}$ immediately departs from.

With a **fixed** decoder, the latent coordinates have a stable, data-independent meaning:
- In the Hermite basis, $h_1$ always means "amount of horizontal shift," $h_2$ always means "amount of vertical shift," etc.
- In the PCA basis, $h_1$ always means "projection onto the first principal component of log-profile variation."

This stability is what makes it meaningful to say "profiles in resolution bin 5 typically have $h_1 \approx 2.3 \pm 0.8$" — the statement remains true throughout training because the coordinate system doesn't change.

The cost of fixing $\mathbf{W}$ is that the basis may not be optimal for reconstruction. However, for well-behaved Bragg profiles, both Hermite and PCA bases capture the relevant variation efficiently in $d \lesssim 30$ dimensions, and the reconstruction error from the fixed basis is small compared to Poisson counting noise.
